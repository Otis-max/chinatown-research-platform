Chinese Exclusion Act Historical Timeline

Records the legislative and policy changes against Chinese people in American history

1848
California Gold Rush
The Gold Rush triggered a wave of global migration, attracting a large number of laborers from Guangdong, China. The first group of Chinese arrived in San Francisco and mainly worked as miners, railroad workers, laundrymen, and cooks—low-paid and labor-intensive jobs. This wave of immigration laid the demographic and cultural foundation for the formation of Chinatowns across the United States and marked the collective emergence of Chinese people in American history.
1854
People v. Hall (California Supreme Court Case)
In this case, the California Supreme Court ruled that Chinese people could not testify in court against white people. This decision deprived the Chinese of their most basic legal rights, leaving them unable to defend themselves through the legal system when facing violence, bullying, or discrimination. It not only opened the door to legalized racial discrimination, but also became the legal foundation for a series of later discriminatory laws.
1871
Los Angeles Chinese Massacre
Due to racial prejudice and hostility from white laborers, a mob stormed into Chinatown in Los Angeles and brutally killed at least 17 Chinese individuals, looting and burning homes and shops. Although the violence occurred publicly, no one was convicted. This incident revealed the deep hostility toward Chinese people in society and the complicity of the justice system, and it also prompted Chinese communities to strengthen self-protection and organization.
1882
Chinese Exclusion Act Enacted
The act prohibited Chinese laborers from entering the United States for 10 years and explicitly stated that Chinese people could not naturalize as American citizens. This was the first immigration restriction law in U.S. history targeting a specific ethnic group. Rooted in racism and economic competition, it directly led to a sharp decline in Chinese immigration, shrinking Chinatown populations, and had long-term negative impacts on Sino-American relations.
1885
Rock Springs Massacre
In Rock Springs, Wyoming, white miners, angry over wages and work assignments, turned their resentment toward Chinese laborers and launched a large-scale violent attack. A total of 28 Chinese immigrants were killed, 15 injured, and 79 houses burned. Although federal troops later restored order, no one was prosecuted. This event became another typical case of anti-Chinese violence and highlighted the severity of systemic racial hatred in American society.
1888
Scott Act Enacted
Building on the existing Chinese Exclusion Act, this act further strengthened restrictions by explicitly prohibiting Chinese laborers who had obtained re-entry permits from returning to the United States. As a result, more than 20,000 Chinese laborers temporarily visiting family in China were denied re-entry, and many families were permanently separated. This act deepened the harm to the Chinese community both legally and emotionally, showing that U.S. exclusion policy had entered a ruthless and absolute stage.
1892
Geary Act Passed
This act extended the 1882 Chinese Exclusion Act for another 10 years and for the first time required Chinese residents in the United States to carry a Certificate of Residence. Those without the certificate would face arrest and deportation. This measure triggered widespread resistance and nonviolent protests in the Chinese community, but also marked the establishment of intensive surveillance and discriminatory governance over Chinese society by the U.S. government.
1904
Peak: Chinese Exclusion Made Permanent
All Chinese labor immigration was permanently banned, with only a few exempt groups (such as merchants and students) allowed to enter. This policy nearly completely cut off the legal pathway for Chinese immigration, leading to aging and structural stagnation in Chinatown populations and further deepening the isolation and marginalization of Chinese people in American society.
1943
Historical turning point: Magnuson Act Signed
During World War II, in order to gain China's support as an ally, the U.S. government repealed the 1882 Chinese Exclusion Act in 1943 and enacted the Magnuson Act, allowing 105 Chinese immigrants to enter legally each year and permitting Chinese people to naturalize as U.S. citizens for the first time. Although the quota was very low, this act symbolized the formal end of the exclusionary policy and was an important victory in the Chinese community's struggle for equal rights.
The official end of the Chinese Exclusion Policy
2011
Formal Apology by the U.S. Senate
The U.S. Senate passed Resolution 201, formally apologizing to the Chinese community for the Chinese Exclusion Act and related laws implemented in the 19th and early 20th centuries. The resolution acknowledged that the legislation was "inconsistent with American values." Although the apology came late, it represented official recognition of historical injustice and an important step toward reflection and reconciliation in American society.
